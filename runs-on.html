What the legal work runs on

In-house contract work runs on eight kinds of knowledge. Two arrive from the outside world. Two the department already holds. One has to be written by its lawyers. And three vanish as the work happens unless they're caught. That last group is the whole conversation.

The eight kinds

Four fates

Every kind of knowledge the work depends on is in one of four states. The state tells you what it costs to have it. The full map names the same states in data terms, one level more precise.

From outside

2 of 8
The law · What's market

Kept current by the outside world. Nothing here is unique to any one company, and none of it needs a lawyer's time to maintain.

Already in house

2 of 8
The company's signed agreements · Who can sign and approve

Everything the company ever agreed sits locked in documents until it's extracted into data. Signing authority sits scattered across systems and has to be read fresh from the systems that own it, never trusted stale, so a lapsed approval is never relied on as current.

Written, or it doesn't exist

1 of 8
The department's positions, and the reasons behind them

The playbook is legal judgment written down: what the department accepts, where it falls back, when it walks. A playbook stored as prose in a PDF is not yet data. Systems can draft positions from redline history; deciding what the department stands behind is the part only its lawyers can do.

Vanishing as the work happens

3 of 8
Why the deal matters · What happened with this party before · What happened after signing

These exist for a moment, in a thread or a head. Either they're caught then, or someone excavates email months later, at cost, without the verbal layer, and without a record anyone can trust. They are also the three kinds every intelligent step depends on most.

The leak

Four feeds lost every day

The work itself produces the missing knowledge. It has nowhere to land, so it evaporates. Each fix is a field or an event on a surface the team already has. The field is the cheap part; getting it filled under deal pressure is the real build.

Lost at intake

Why the deal matters

Known on day one, gone by drafting. The rationale, the stakes, and what the business needs from it live in an email thread.

The fix: three fields on the request · rationale, stakes, walk-away
Lost in negotiation

What was conceded, and why

Every negotiation teaches the team how this party behaves and what the terms are worth. Today that lesson retires with the deal team.

The fix: one structured line per concession
Lost at approval

Why the exception got a yes

The approval survives; the reasoning doesn't. So the same exception gets re-argued from scratch next quarter.

The fix: a reason field on the approval record
Never recorded

What happened after signing

Disputes, renewals lost, clauses that failed in practice. The one feed that would make every playbook and every renewal call better, and nobody keeps it.

The fix: outcome tags at renewal and dispute
What it takes

Three commitments

1

Lawyers write the playbook as records, and grade outcomes

Positions with their reasons, versioned and owned. And a lawyer's call on whether each outcome was good. Systems can draft both; standing behind them is legal judgment, and that stays with the lawyers.

2

The platform gives the vanishing feeds somewhere to land

Fields and events on surfaces that already exist: the request, the approval, the renewal. The schema is the easy part; each field also needs an owner, a shared vocabulary, and a reason to fill it in that survives deal pressure.

3

Everything captured sits under a privilege model from day one

What may be recorded, who may see it, how long it lives. Records kept light enough to prove diligence without storing the privileged substance. A capture program without these rules is a discovery program.
