What the legal work runs on

Every step of contract work draws on knowledge from somewhere: the law, the department's positions, its own signed paper, what happened last time. And every one of those kinds of knowledge runs, in turn, on data that either exists today or doesn't. This page names the eight kinds, shows which steps use which, and specifies the data each one requires, so legal and product can see what has to exist before any step can be made intelligent.

01
The idea

Intelligence is the input to every step, and data is the input to intelligence

Ask why a contract step needs a lawyer and the answer is always some piece of knowledge: what the law requires, what the position is, what was agreed with this party before, who is allowed to sign. Teams usually treat all of that as one pile called experience. It separates into eight kinds. Each kind then depends on specific records: some already exist and stay maintained, some lie inside the department's own documents waiting to be extracted, some have to be written by lawyers, and some exist only for the moment the work happens and are gone unless captured. The build conversation gets concrete the moment the kinds and their data are named.

Scope: this maps the transactional-contract function, the high-volume repeatable-agreement work. Disputes, employment, M&A, and IP run on different intelligence.

06
The byproduct

The steps produce data too

Six steps generate intelligence that later steps need, and today most of it evaporates into email and memory. Then negotiation runs without counterparty memory and renewal runs without outcome data. Each capture is a field or an event on an existing surface, nothing platform-sized.

Step 01 · Intake

The why

Deal rationale, stakes, the internal client's real intent. Used again at triage, drafting, and escalation.

Capture: structured why-fields on the request record
Step 07 · Redline

Deviation decisions

Which departures from the playbook the team accepted, and why. Used again at the next redline of the same clause.

Capture: a deviation event per accepted departure
Step 08 · Negotiation

Concession history

What was conceded, to whom, in exchange for what. The verbal layer no document captures. Used again at the next negotiation with the same party.

Capture: one structured line per concession
Step 09 · Escalation

Exception rationales

Why the exception was granted. Used again the next time the same exception is requested.

Capture: rationale text on the approval record
Step 10 · Signature

The authorization record

Who was authorized to bind, and did. The one emission already kept as data.

Already captured: the completion record
Step 12 · Obligations

Performance events

Whether both sides delivered, and where it hurt. Used again at the renewal call.

Capture: outcome tags linked to agreement and clause IDs
07
The people

Who maintains what

Lawyers

Own outright: the playbook with its rationale, because writing positions is legal judgment. And outcome grading, because deciding whether a result was good is legal judgment.

Curate: what counts as market, how big the stakes are, whether a counterparty position is real. The platform holds these; a lawyer labels the judgment inside them.

The platform

Everything else: extraction coverage, data freshness, the wiring to source systems, and the capture surfaces where the six emissions land as events instead of email.

The playbook owner and the outcome grader are the two lawyer roles this map creates. Both are faces of the legal engineer.

Governance before capture

Business context, counterparty memory, and outcome data are the most useful kinds and the most sensitive. Before any capture surface ships, put it under a privilege model: what may be recorded, who may see it, how long it lives. And keep records light enough to prove diligence without storing the privileged substance. A capture program without these rules is a discovery program.
